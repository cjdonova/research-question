üéì Understanding Research Topics vs. Questions

A Research Topic is:

  • A statement that describes the focus of your study
  • Broad enough to encompass multiple potential questions
  • The what you want to study
  • Example: "The relationship between social media use and sleep quality among college students"

A Research Question is:

  • An interrogative sentence that asks what you want to find out
  • Specific and answerable through research
  • The how you will investigate your topic
  • Example: "Is there an association between daily social media use duration and sleep quality scores among college students?"

üìä Example Process

Setting: High schools in urban areas

Result: "The effects of social media use on anxiety levels among adolescents in urban high schools"

Evaluate Your Question

üîç Quality Checklist

Click each item to learn more about what it means, then check it off if your question meets that criterion:

‚òê Does my question allow for multiple possible answers? +

What this means: Your question shouldn't have just one obvious answer or be a yes/no question. Good research questions are open-ended and can be explored from different angles.

‚úÖ Good: "What is the relationship between social media use and anxiety levels among teenagers?"

‚ùå Bad: "Is social media bad for teenagers?" (too simple, yes/no answer)

‚òê Is it testable with available research methods? +

What this means: You should be able to imagine how researchers could actually study this question using surveys, experiments, data analysis, or observations.

‚úÖ Good: "How effective are workplace wellness programs in reducing employee stress levels?" (can measure stress, compare groups)

‚ùå Bad: "What do people think in their hearts about health?" (too abstract to measure)

‚òê Is the question clear and precise? +

What this means: Someone else reading your question should understand exactly what you're asking. Avoid vague terms that could mean different things to different people.

‚úÖ Good: "What is the prevalence of food insecurity among college students at public universities?"

‚ùå Bad: "How do people feel about health issues?" (what people? which feelings? what health issues?)

‚òê Is it suitable for quantitative analysis? +

What this means: Since this is for quantitative research, your question should involve things that can be counted, measured, or statistically analyzed.

‚úÖ Good: "Is there an association between exercise frequency and BMI levels?" (can count exercise sessions, measure BMI)

‚ùå Bad: "What does wellness mean to different people?" (too subjective for statistical analysis)

‚òê Can I find peer-reviewed articles on this topic? +

What this means: Your question should be about a topic that researchers have studied before. You need to find 3 peer-reviewed articles for your assignment.

‚úÖ Good: Questions about vaccination, obesity, mental health, or substance use (lots of research available)

‚ùå Bad: Very new trends or extremely specific local issues (limited research available)

üí° Tip: Do a quick search on PubMed or Google Scholar to check!

‚òê Does it avoid being too broad or too narrow? +

What this means: Your question should be focused enough to study deeply, but broad enough that research exists and you can find patterns.

‚úÖ Just right: "How does access to telehealth services affect preventive care utilization among rural elderly adults?"

‚ùå Too broad: "What affects health?" (could be anything)

‚ùå Too narrow: "Do 23-year-old male nursing students in Ohio prefer morning or evening exercise?" (too specific)

‚òê Would the answer contribute to public health knowledge? +

What this means: Your research should help us understand health issues better or inform public health practice and policy.

‚úÖ Good: "What factors are associated with COVID-19 vaccination uptake among rural adults?" (could inform health campaigns)

‚ùå Less useful: "What's the favorite color of people who exercise?" (interesting but not really about health)

üí° Think: Could this research help improve health outcomes or guide public health interventions?

üéØ Final Question Review

üîß Common Problems to Avoid

Too broad: "What affects health?" ‚Üí Better: "How does access to green spaces affect mental health in urban adults?"

Embedded assumption: "Why do health programs fail?" ‚Üí Better: "What is the effectiveness of school nutrition programs?"
